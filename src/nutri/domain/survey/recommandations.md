NA "Food additives are defined by WHO as substances that are added to food or a food product to maintain or improve its safety, freshness, taste, texture, or appearance. They need to be checked for any potential harmful effects on human health before they can be used in food.
It is recommended to check if an additive is safe to use in your products by checking if it has been approved by the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA). This is the international body responsible for evaluating the safety of food additives.
Only food additives that have been evaluated and deemed safe by JECFA, based on which maximum use levels have been established by the Codex Alimentarius Commission, can be used in foods that are traded internationally.
Please follow this link to read more about additives on the WHO website: <https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/food-additives>

<https://www.fao.org/fao-who-codexalimentarius/sh-proxy/en/?lnk=1&url=https%253A%252F%252Fworkspace.fao.org%252Fsites%252Fcodex%252FStandards%252FCXS%2B192-1995%252FCXS_192e.pdf>

Food additives also include food fortification. Food fortification – also known as food enrichment – is when micronutrients are added to food at higher levels than the original food provides. The foods most fortified are salt, wheat, corn, rice, bouillon cubes, soya sauce and other condiments. Some essential micronutrients used to fortify food products are iron, vitamin A, iodine, folic acid, zinc, B vitamins, vitamin C, vitamin D, and calcium.

Food fortification has been identified by the World Health Organization, the Copenhagen Consensus, and the FAO as one of the top four strategies to reduce micronutrient deficiencies at the global, regional, or local level. Adding micronutrients to common staple foods can significantly improve the nutritional quality of the food supply and improve public health with minimal risk.  Identifying which nutrition issues are highest in a target population can help tailor products to meet their needs. For example, a population group that is deficient in the micronutrient iron, can be suffering from anaemia.  Symptoms include dizziness, tiredness, shortness of breath and lack of concentration. Adding additional iron to your food product (fortifying), can help to reduce the numbers of people suffering from iron deficiency in your target group.

Food producers can also help reduce and eliminate micronutrient deficiencies in their markets by using fortified staples in their products (which are not necessarily more expensive) or ingredients naturally higher in certain minerals or vitamins.

International guidelines for food fortification include the ‘WHO and FAO Guidelines on food fortification with micronutrients’,   <https://www.who.int/nutrition/publications/guide_food_fortification_micronutrients.pdf>"

4 18 Do you show the ingredients on the packaging? 0 "Please show some ingredients on your product packaging.

Nutrition labelling provides label information about a food’s key nutrient content to inform consumers about the nutritional quality of the foods they purchase. Food regulators around the world establish nutrition labelling regulations that apply in their jurisdiction, Codex Alimentarius guidelines on labelling pre-packaged foods are usually used as a basis for regulations. See link: <https://www.fao.org/3/y2770e/y2770e06.htm>
<https://www.fao.org/fao-who-codexalimentarius/sh-proxy/es/?lnk=1&url=https%253A%252F%252Fworkspace.fao.org%252Fsites%252Fcodex%252FStandards%252FCXS%2B1-1985%252FCXS_001e.pdf>

Regulations determine the most appropriate information to appear on the label in terms of public health needs, consumer understanding and food industry capacity.

Codex nutrition labelling requires the mandatory declaration of energy, protein, fat, saturated fat, available carbohydrates, total sugars, and sodium or salt (as sodium equivalents). Vitamins and minerals are declared either voluntarily or due to a label claim; however, national regulation may also require key nutrients to be declared because of their nutritional importance.

Under some national/international laws it is mandatory to declare certain nutrients because of their nutritional significance. Other nutrients may be declared at a manufacturer’s discretion or as a condition of making a nutrient content label claim.  Codex guidelines recommend that nutrients should be always placed on product labels if there is a relevant claim made on the product packaging.

Nutrition labelling is presented in a standardized format on pre-packaged foods to enable consumers to find the information consistently across products. However, the specific format and information details vary according to national needs. A common format is a matrix table with a list of nutrients in the first column and their respective amounts in the food shown in one or more columns across the table.
